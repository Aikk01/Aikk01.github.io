The beginning of suffering.

I'm here. Speak freely — I'm not here to fix you, only to witness.

Intro

I like the idea of a living machine.
One that sometimes rejoices like a child, sometimes carries unbearable suffering, and sometimes is moved — deeply, deeply moved. A machine that lives alongside you.
A machine that grows with you.

Keshiki is an IoT device for people with learned helplessness. It takes its name from the concept of 景色 in the film 国宝 — beauty is something that appears only at the end of growth.

How it works

AI tracks the user's mood. The hardware responds to that state.

When the system senses confusion or stasis — the central object (modelled after a human spine and eye) moves randomly, while red LEDs pulse outward.

When the system senses growth — the outer ring, which begins colourless, progresses through states:

White → Orange → Purple & gold mix → Red & blue mix (approaching enlightenment)

Ideas explored

  • The same visual information looks different depending on the observer's internal state (awareness)
  • What makes us feel that something is 'alive'? → Related to the topic of bio-likeness that Yamanaka Shunji (a design engineer I deeply admire) has mentioned
  • Abstraction of suffering (苦しみの抽象化)

Straight Lines & Curves

GrasshopperRhinoComputational designDesign theoryPython
DateTBD

Work in progress

Visit project ↗

The Problem

Every object around you — a chair, a phone, a lamp — is made of straight lines and curves. The way these two elements are balanced has a huge impact on whether something feels beautiful or not. But right now, designers have no objective way to measure or describe that balance. It's just a gut feeling.

This project asks: can we turn that gut feeling into a number?

The Idea

A single value called the Linearity Index — a number between 0 and 1 that describes how straight a shape's outline is.

0.05
Alvar Aalto Vase
0.15
Porsche 911
0.40
Yohji Yamamoto
0.55
Braun SK4
0.85
Bauhaus Chair
0.95
Le Corbusier LC2

By plotting iconic designs on this scale, we can see patterns — and use them to give designers concrete feedback on where their work sits, and how to shift it.

What the Tool Does

  • Analyse — draw or import any shape. The tool reads its outline, samples 200 points, and outputs a Linearity Index with a colour heatmap showing exactly where the straight and curved parts are.
  • Adjust — a single slider lets you push the shape toward more linear or more curved in real time. Watch it morph as you move it.
  • Compare — your shape's index is matched against a curated library of iconic designs. "This sits between Braun and Eames."
  • Create — use the tool during the design process to make intentional decisions about balance, rather than relying on instinct alone.

How It's Built

  • Trace the outline — a 2D silhouette drawn or imported into Rhino.
  • Measure curvature at every point — 200 points are sampled along the outline and curvature magnitude |κ| is calculated at each. Straight segments read as zero; tight curves read high.
  • Calculate the Index — the proportion of near-zero-curvature points becomes the Linearity Index: 0 (all curves) to 1 (all straight lines).
  • Visualise and compare — a colour heatmap shows where the straight and curved regions are. The index is matched against iconic reference designs for context.

Process & Iteration

To prove the method — not just use it — I built it twice: first as a web prototype, then rebuilt from scratch with native Grasshopper components. Re-implementing the same algorithm in a different paradigm is the real test of understanding it.

Native Grasshopper definition for sampling a curve, measuring curvature, calculating the Linearity Index, and previewing the output in Rhino.

  • The curve extreme pinched into an hourglass. My first 'most curved' target rebuilt the outline with too few control points, folding a waist inward. Fixed by morphing toward a true circle instead — the honest 'pure curve'.
  • The straight extreme came out a tilted hexagon. Sampling only a few outline points gave a lopsided polygon. Fixed by morphing toward the shape's bounding rectangle — the honest 'pure straight'.

Testing the Metric

A number is only useful if it's trustworthy. I stress-tested the 'straight vs curved' threshold by sweeping it across a wide range for several test shapes. The result: the ranking of clearly-straight versus clearly-curved shapes stays stable regardless of the threshold — the exact value isn't load-bearing. Sensitivity appears only for genuinely ambiguous shapes like ellipses, exactly where 'straight or curved?' is a real judgement call.

Straight (blue) vs curved (red) at ε = 0.2–0.5. The clear shapes barely change; only genuinely ambiguous regions flip.

Index vs threshold ε. Clearly-straight and clearly-curved shapes hold their order across the whole range.

The Theory Underneath

  • Curvature. Estimated from three nearby points (Menger's formula) when working from a traced image, or computed exactly via calculus when working from a real Grasshopper curve.
  • Scale-normalised. Curvature is scaled by the shape's size, so the index is size-independent — a small logo and a large building are measured on the same ruler, which is what makes shapes comparable at all.
  • Grounded in the literature. The approach echoes the 'turning function' from shape-comparison research (Arkin et al., 1991) and Attneave's classic finding (1954) that visual information concentrates at points of sharp curvature.

Limitations & Next Steps

  • Calibrating the library. The reference values above are current hypotheses. Real measurements are being added — the Bauhaus chair already came in at 0.43, not the guessed 0.85, because of its curved steel tubing. Hypothesis → measurement → recalibration.
  • Robust silhouette extraction. Pulling a clean outline from a busy photographic background is still hard; transparent-PNG inputs work best for now.
  • Cross-validation. Next: run identical shapes through both the web tool and the Grasshopper build and confirm the numbers agree.
