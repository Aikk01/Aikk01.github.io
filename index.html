Keshiki

ESP32NeoPixelPETG/PLA 3D printServoSolderingUI/UX web design
DateTBD

Work in progress

Keshiki is my take on making an object that grows with us. It is an IoT device for people with learned helplessness. It takes its name from the concept of 景色 in the film 国宝 — the idea that visual beauty is something that appears only at the end of growth.

Context

For a long time, technology has been used mainly to complete tasks more efficiently, and productivity has kept rising across the world. Yet, in parallel, so have rates of depression.

With the arrival of artificial intelligence — and the move toward artificial general intelligence — many people have begun using generative AI simply to talk, and there is growing attention on the role of technology in mental health.

The problem

Is technology not helping us reach our supposedly ultimate goal — to be happier?

Hypothesis

Can hardware and AI be combined to create an object that portrays growth — the way humans do?

Background to the hypothesis

Something similar to a robotic pet with integrated AI?

Solution

I like the idea of a living machine. One that sometimes rejoices like a child, sometimes carries unbearable suffering, and sometimes is moved — deeply, deeply moved — just like any human. A machine that lives alongside you.

  1. AI tracks the user's mood, and the hardware responds to that state.
  2. When the system senses confusion or stasis, the central object — modelled after a human spine and eye — moves randomly, while red LEDs pulse in sequence to portray suffering.
  3. When the system senses growth, the outer ring — which begins colourless — progresses through states: White → Orange → Purple & gold mix → Red & blue mix (approaching enlightenment).
→ → → →

The beginning of suffering.

I'm here. Speak freely — I'm not here to fix you, only to witness.

Future steps

  • Finish the prototype
  • Explore how the same visual information can look different depending on personal state
  • Explore what makes us feel that something is 'alive' → related to 'bio-likeness', a topic raised by Yamanaka Shunji, a design engineer I deeply admire
  • Explore the abstraction of suffering

Application of the tool

To be explored as the prototype matures.

Project description

Straight and Curved Lines

GrasshopperRhinoComputational designDesign theoryPython
DateJune 2026
Visit project ↗

The Linearity Index aims to measure the composition ratio of straight lines to curves as a continuous quantity, shape by shape. It is a web tool that can predict, visually, how a given shape would look if it were more linear or more curved. To remove the black box in the code and make it easier for designers to handle, I implemented the same results as the web tool in Grasshopper.

Context

Humans are visually dominant creatures. And I think many people consider visual beauty an important factor in their quality of life.

The Problem

Yet the logical explanation of what humans find beautiful seems to lag far behind the importance of the problem.

The Hypothesis

I thought that one factor determining visual beauty might be the ratio of straight lines to curves.

Background to the Hypothesis

In The Analysis of Beauty (1753), Hogarth argued that a moderate curvature is optimal, and that being too flat or undulating too much both spoil beauty. Bar & Neta (2006), a landmark study that tested that intuition with modern psychology experiments, propose that sharp-angled contours, linked to threat and danger, feel unpleasant, while curved contours, linked to the absence of danger and to reward, feel pleasant.

However, most existing work on the ‘beauty’ of straight lines and curves stops at a binary comparison — curve or angle. Among the few studies that engage with the ‘amount’ of curvature, even the inverted-U relationship between curvature level and preference reported by Ho, Lu & Chen (2016, International Journal of Design) measures the ‘degree of bending’, not the ‘proportion’ of straight to curved within a single contour. While there are studies that manipulate complexity via vertex count or concavity ratio, examples that quantify linearity itself as a continuous quantity are almost nowhere to be found.

Why I Chose This Solution

The importance of the straight-to-curve ratio is suggested in industrial products. Naoto Fukasawa, the MUJI designer, has said that the closer a product is to people, the rounder he designs it.

Solution Explanation

  1. Loading the image and extracting the outline.
  2. The outline is divided into 200 points, and curvature (Menger curvature) is calculated from three points (i-K, i, i+K). Curvature is shown in colour (blue: straight, red: curved). The proportion of points that can be regarded as straight is then computed as a value between 0 and 1.
  3. The shape can be made more linear or more curved.

Step 1 runs in the web tool; steps 2 and 3 give the same results in both the web tool and Grasshopper.

Step 1 in detail — outline extraction

  • ① Importing and downscaling the image. The image is downscaled so its longest side is at most 420px (to lighten processing and suppress the effect of noise). To support transparent PNGs, the background is first filled with white before the image is drawn. Only the subject remains.
  • ② Grayscale conversion. The colour image is converted to a single brightness (luminance) channel. Formula: 0.299×R + 0.587×G + 0.114×B (weighted to match the sensitivity of the human eye).
  • ③ Automatic threshold estimation (Otsu's method). From the brightness histogram, the brightness boundary that most cleanly separates foreground from background is computed automatically. This value becomes the initial value of the threshold slider (which the user can fine-tune by hand).
  • ④ Binarisation (splitting into black and white). Each pixel is sorted into ‘darker than the threshold = shape (1) / lighter = background (0)’.
  • ⑤ Automatic foreground/background detection (inversion). If more than half of the image's four edges (border) are ON, the foreground and background are judged to be reversed and the black/white is inverted (the design lets the subject be extracted correctly on either a dark or a light background).
  • ⑥ Outline extraction (marching squares). d3.contours traces the boundary line (contour) of the black-and-white mask. If several contours appear, the longest (= largest) contour is adopted. This becomes the ‘outline (silhouette)’.

Native Grasshopper definition for sampling a curve, measuring curvature, calculating the Linearity Index, and previewing the output in Rhino.

Justifying the Threshold

Straight (blue) vs curved (red) at ε = 0.2–0.5. The clear shapes barely change; only genuinely ambiguous regions flip.

Index vs threshold ε. Clearly-straight and clearly-curved shapes hold their order across the whole range.

To find the best value across the range of the 'straight or curved' threshold, I tested several shapes. As a result, the ranking of clearly-straight versus clearly-curved shapes was stable regardless of the threshold. A difference appears only for inherently ambiguous shapes like the ellipse — exactly where the 'straight or curved?' judgement is split. For this build I adopted a threshold of 0.3, which treats the ellipse as a perfect curve.

Future steps

I want to feed different products into the tool and find the rules behind the shapes people find beautiful.

Application of the Tool

I hope it will be useful in product development.
